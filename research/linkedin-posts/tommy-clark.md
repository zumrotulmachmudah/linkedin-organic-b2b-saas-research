## Post 1 — "How to vet a LinkedIn content agency" (repost)
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/tclarkmedia_i-take-5-10-calls-per-week-with-b2b-founders-activity-7469800167378931712-hoxi
- **Date:** ~2026-06-08 (posted "1w" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 61 likes, 10 comments
- **Note:** Repost of Post 8 (originally published ~4 months ago). A live example of his own advice to "repurpose old winners."

I take ~5-10 calls per week with B2B founders who want to post more on LinkedIn. I'd say ~50% of them have tried working with a content agency before. It didn't go well.

So, they end up at our doorstep.

You don't have to go through this pain. If you're going to work with a content agency on your personal brand…run them through this gauntlet.

(1) Ask who will actually be writing for your account.

If the agency is oddly low-priced (and it's an agency you're working with, not a freelancer), you're probably getting passed off to a junior writer—or every agency bro's favorite new coworker, Claude.

Get the name of the person owning your account. Meet them. If the founder or salesperson balks at that, I'd be wary of that agency.

(2) See if the founder is obsessed with content.

This seems silly. But plenty of people start LinkedIn ghostwriting agencies for the promise of easy money (LOL - different post topic).

It's in your best interest to work with an agency whose leadership loves the craft of writing and comms.

(3) Observe whether the agency is qualifying you.

We say 'NO' more often than we say 'YES.' Savvy agencies know which clients they're best equipped to support. They aren't afraid to turn folks away.

(4) Understand how they adapt to your tone of voice.

There are plenty of people who can ghostwrite LinkedIn content based on generic best practices. Hell, even Claude can.

But they tend to lack the ability to adapt away from LinkedIn 'broetry' and formulaic hooks. In my experience, founders are particular about their tone. Rightfully so.

(5) Make sure you like each other as people.

I cannot stress how important this is. For a founder-led content engagement to work, you'll need to sit down with these folks on a biweekly basis and communicate over Slack almost daily.

Lack of vibe creates friction. That friction ruins the content creation workflow.

Hope this helps. If you have any questions, lmk in the comments or drop me a note.

## Post 2 — "2 ways founders win on LinkedIn in 2026" (repost)
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/tclarkmedia_i-spend-most-of-my-days-on-linkedin-or-talking-activity-7467275544431276033-7Av8
- **Date:** ~2026-06-01 (posted "2w" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 41 likes, 13 comments
- **Note:** Near-identical repost of Post 5 (originally published ~2 months ago, 57 likes/13 comments). Minor wording change: "tech founders" here vs "founders and executives" in the original.

I spend most of my days on LinkedIn, or talking LinkedIn with clients. It's gotten harder, no doubt. There are 2 ways I see tech founders win on this platform in 2026.

(1) Pairing each 'take' with a personal story or anecdote.

AI has commoditized 'value' content. I could get away with posting something like 'Here are five tips for B2B founders to grow on LinkedIn' a couple of years back. Not now. Value content still works, but you have to deliver that value with something else: a story, tasteful humor, etc.

(2) Sharing unique, proprietary data that only they have access to.

Some of the best content on LinkedIn these days is data visualizations. The team at Ramp does a great job of this. Peter Walker at Carta is another example.

Candidly, this is an area I'd like to improve at Compound. We have 150+ posts per week going live across our clients. I should be sharing more specific learnings from that data. Perhaps I'll have time to do this once we hire our new VP of Ops, lol.

If we look back 2 years on this platform, the most successful accounts were already doing this. But back then, it was more of a 'luxury.'

Now, to cut through the noise of AI slop and poorly ghostwritten stuff, you've got to have one of these two elements in your content.

Run your next post through this filter. I think you'll be happy with the results.

## Post 3 — "What's the best posting cadence on LinkedIn?"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/tclarkmedia_whats-the-best-posting-cadence-on-linkedin-activity-7452360599705120768-DOuu
- **Date:** ~2026-05-15 (posted "1 month" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 60 likes, 26 comments

What's the "best" posting cadence on LinkedIn? A lot of the tech executives I talk to about content strategy are nervous about posting 5x per week. I used to be very dismissive of this; but I've since softened a bit...

I'm still bullish on an aggressive cadence. 3-5x per week gets you more exposure to your audience, and more 'shots on goal' to see what's hitting.

But if every post sounds the same, and looks the same, you risk your audience tuning you out as 'just another content creator.'

The simple fix for this is to CHANGE IT UP! It's so so so simple. One day, post a long-form 'value-based' listicle. The next day, post a short-form hot take. The next, a meme. A carousel. A short-form vertical video. Then another long-form post. You can vary ad infinitum. Yet, every exec thinks 'posting on LinkedIn' means a long-form, educational text-based post.

I also think it is OK to post less in a week if you have less to say. Forgive me for the unironic 48 Laws of Power reference, but Robert Greene does make a point when he advises in Law 16 to 'use absence to increase respect and honor.'

There is a time to thug it out and repost an old winner or write a quick, scrappy piece to get a post up, but I do believe varying posting cadence along with format lets your audience know you're human.

## Post 4 — "Founders who need to be 'convinced' to post likely never will"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/tclarkmedia_if-i-were-a-head-of-marketing-of-a-hot-b2b-activity-7450944325766660096-VUeB
- **Date:** ~2026-05-15 (posted "1 month" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 83 likes, 32 comments

If I were a Head of Marketing of a hot B2B AI startup fresh off a fat funding round, trying to convince the CEO to finally start posting founder-led content on LinkedIn, here's what I'd do:

I wouldn't.

Founders who need to be 'convinced' to take content seriously likely never will.

I've had dozens and dozens of these conversations over the course of the last three years building my agency. Head of Marketing comes inbound. I take the sales call. They mention the founder thinks posting LinkedIn content is cringe and doubts it will work for them...

Earlier on in the agency's lifecycle, I'd have spent more time and energy convincing them of the platform's benefit.

Sometimes I'd succeed, but the victory would be a Pyrrhic one when 3 months into the relationship, nobody's happy.

Now, this mindset is an immediate disqualifier.

Let the CEO come to realize that content matters on their own time. Some never will, and that's fine. But to win on LinkedIn, you need to enjoy the game at least a little bit.

I'm sure there are exceptions. I've yet to find them.

## Post 6 — "The debate about posting frequency affecting LinkedIn reach is ridiculous"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/tclarkmedia_the-debate-about-whether-or-not-posting-frequency-activity-7440048295915388928-2s_P
- **Date:** ~2026-04-15 (posted "2 months" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 39 likes, 13 comments

The debate about whether or not posting frequency affects your LinkedIn reach is ridiculous.
The truth:

Most marketers will fit their opinion on this around their current preferences or abilities.

The ideal posting cadence is as often as you can without a drop-off in quality.

There is NOTHING wrong with posting daily....if the content is good.

The problem is, most marketers who try to ramp up cadence for their execs' content don't have the skill or infrastructure in place to support it. Then they try to tell you the algorithm screwed them. Sure.

A few tactical notes on keeping quality high while posting 5-7x per week:

(1) Quality and length are not as correlated as you think. Some of my best pieces took the least amount of time to write.

(2) Balance 'in-depth' thought leadership with 'easy wins' like team highlights, hiring posts, and company milestones. I like to aim for 1-2 more thought-out content pieces per week. The rest can be low-lift, and still get you a ton of visibility.

(3) During busy weeks, repurpose old winners. You can reword them slightly, or even copy-paste an outlier post from months back. I've done this more than once. Usually hits.

To be clear: you don't *have* to post 5x per week on LinkedIn to 'get the algorithm to favor you.' That's BS. All it is is simple math. If you post more often, you have more shots on goal for an outlier post. The savviest creators have systems for keeping output high without a sacrifice in quality.

## Post 7 — "Every B2B startup founder should try LinkedIn content — here's a simple guide"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/tclarkmedia_every-b2b-startup-founder-should-try-their-activity-7424505469936930816-ffGF
- **Date:** ~2026-02-15 (posted "4 months" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 42 likes, 12 comments

Every B2B startup founder should try their hand at LinkedIn content. It's silly not to. Here's why, what you should post about, and how to get started (in one simple guide).

Btw - I've helped 75+ founders & CEOs get started with social content and I've used content to grow my own company to $1M+ in revenue last year (2nd year in biz).

Ok, so why bother posting?

- You'll get more inbound leads (duh)
- You'll have an easier time sourcing A+ to join your company
- You'll get more inbound investors wanting to give you money
- You'll make your other marketing channels (outbound, paid media, etc) perform better

That's really it.

WTF do you post about?

Simple framework - find Content-Market Fit.

- What does your ICP care about?
- What's relevant to your product?
- What do you have the credibility to write about?

Once you've landed on one core focus…Not 5. Not 3. 1. Then you'll build your Content Funnel.

- TOFU: broader "founder story" and company-building content that's optimized for reach
- MOFU: industry-specific thought leadership that positions you as a go-to resource for your ICP
- BOFU: product content - case studies, feature highlights, all the stuff that pitches your software

For example - say you're selling a SaaS in the GTM tech category selling into founders and revenue leaders. And you were a former VP of Sales at a well-known company like Gong. Easy money.

You'd want to position yourself as a go-to resource in sales & GTM. Your top of funnel content would be 'building in public' updates showing the momentum you have as a company. Middle of funnel content would be sales advice and frameworks that your ICP would find helpful. BOFU content is your feature demos.

How often should you post?

- 5x per week. No, it's not too much. Nobody knows who you are. Get in the timeline more.
- Here's your content mix - 1x TOFU, 3x MOFU, 1x BOFU (good starting point)

You'll either write your own stuff (if you're bootstrapping or haven't raised *yet*).

Or, if you've raised your seed or Series A, you could look to bring on an agency to help you produce content (I know a guy). Hiring in-house for content early on is doable, but hard. You're not gonna know what to look for.

Anyway. Follow this guide. Use the other stuff on my profile and in my newsletter as well. You have 100% of what you need to win with content.

It will change the course of your business if you commit to it for a quarter.
