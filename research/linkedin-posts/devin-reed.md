## Post 5 — "Write like a Hollywood screenwriter, not a journalist"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/devinreed_you-shouldnt-write-like-a-journalist-analyst-activity-7459984518205136896-Cw5c
- **Date:** ~2026-05-15 (posted "1 month" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 65 likes, 28 comments

You shouldn't write like a journalist, analyst, or "subject matter expert."
The best marketers write like Hollywood screenwriters.

Because the goal isn't to inform — it's to make your audience *feel* something first, then land the lesson.

Here's the difference in practice:

⛔️ Most B2B content: "Here's what the data says about discovery calls..."

✅ Great content: "You're 5 minutes into a discovery call. Your buyer just said your price is too high. Your palms are sweating. What do you do?"

Same topic. Totally different reaction.
One gets skimmed. The other gets saved.

At Gong, this was our secret weapon. We didn't lead with statistics, benchmarks, or product features.

We led with moments our audience lived every single day — and then delivered the lesson once we had their full attention.

Emotion first. Data second. Every time.

The result? Buyers showed up to demos already trusting us. Sales reps shared our content on their own calls. Pipeline grew without us ever making a single ask.

Your buyers don't want to be educated. They want to feel understood.

Write content that makes them say "that's exactly what I'm going through!" — and they'll never stop reading.

## Post 6 — "If you're a B2B marketer drowning in requests, read this"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/devinreed_if-youre-a-b2b-marketer-drowning-in-requests-activity-7455315156588318720-ZJ3R
- **Date:** ~2026-05-15 (posted "1 month" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 31 likes, 17 comments

If you're a B2B marketer drowning in requests and wondering what to prioritize — read this.

I've led and advised dozens of B2B content teams and the hardest part is never the content itself.

It's knowing where to start when everything feels urgent.

My career changed once I figured out how to handle the overwhelm.

Here's what's helped me:

**1. If your CEO keeps asking you for "one more thing" and your strategy is now a wishlist...**
→ Start with CEO goals, not your content calendar

The first question isn't "what should we create?" It's "what are our top three company priorities — and how does content impact them?" Everything else flows from there. This makes your job easier and your CEO happier.

**2. If you're being asked to create MORE content and wondering how tf am I gonna do that...**
→ Repurpose like hell and build a content waterfall

This is the fastest way to win. One great piece of content — webinar, podcast, report — can fuel LinkedIn posts, blogs, and email all at once. Starting from scratch should be your last resort.

**3. If you're doing too damn much and don't know what's worth keeping...**
→ Measure growth, not just outputs

Pull the data on what's working. If something isn't driving pipeline, audience growth, or brand — kill it or replace it with something better. You could probably stop half your content today and no one would notice. That's not a failure, that's being lean.

I spent years drowning in the same requests. Turns out the answer was never more content — it was knowing exactly what to say no to.

What's the one thing you'd cut from your content strategy tomorrow if you could?

## Post 7 — "How Gong grew from 12K to 220K LinkedIn followers without talking about the product"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/devinreed_when-i-was-at-gong-we-grew-from-12k-220k-activity-7454548671112609792-vCl6
- **Date:** ~2026-05-15 (posted "1 month" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 79 likes, 20 comments

When I was at Gong, we grew from 12K → 220K LinkedIn followers and generated millions in pipeline — without talking about our product once.

Here's the strategy that made it work.

**1. Know your audience better than they know themselves**
We mapped the micro-frustrations sales reps deal with every day — ignored cold emails, awkward discovery calls, pricing objections. Each pain point became content gold.

**2. Be a practitioner, not a product pusher**
Instead of promoting Gong, we focused on helping sellers do their job better. If the content couldn't help someone tomorrow, we didn't publish it.

**3. Lead with story, not statistics**
Data matters, but emotion hooks attention. We opened with real stories sellers recognize before bringing in insights or research.

**4. Show up (nearly) everywhere, consistently**
Blog for readers. Podcasts for listeners. Videos for visual learners. Your audience consumes content differently. Meet them where they are.

**5. Measure like a brand, not just a funnel**
We looked beyond last-touch attribution and tracked signals like follower growth, direct traffic, inbound opportunities, and pipeline influenced by content.

The results:
220K LinkedIn followers
100K podcast downloads
Webinar registrations jumping from 500 → 2,500
Inbound pipeline grew quarter after quarter.

Most marketers fight for existing demand.

The smart ones create it before they need it.
