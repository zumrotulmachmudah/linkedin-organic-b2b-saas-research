## Post 1 — "Algorithmic capital: build it before you spend it"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/amandanat_when-i-talk-about-zero-click-marketing-i-activity-7354525899658055680-2UZK

When I talk about zero-click marketing, I often say you need to build algorithmic capital and spend it wisely. Here's what I mean...

You don't get reach on social media because you deserve it. You get it because you've earned it.

- Every zero-click post (the kind with standalone value and no links)
- Every carousel, every native video, every "ah-ha" moment you deliver
→ Those are algorithmic deposits.

And through your better engagement and reach, you accrue algorithmic capital — trust with your audience and the platform.

That's when you earn the right to make a withdrawal:

💸 "Subscribe to our newsletter."
💸 "Book a demo today."
💸 "Download the guide."

If you're constantly linking out, asking for clicks, or begging for conversions...

Your audience won't love it. And neither will the algorithm.

## Post 3 — "B2B buyers are on LinkedIn... but that's not the whole strategy"

- **Format:** Text + diagram
- **Engagement:** 138 likes, 46 comments, 9 reposts

"B2B buyers are on LinkedIn" is true. It's also unsurprising.

I ran an audience research report for mid-market RevOps leaders at B2B SaaS companies, and as you might've guessed, 80% of this audience uses LinkedIn.

So should LinkedIn be in the plan? Of course.

But the more useful question is:

"Where does this audience find ideas they trust enough to repeat?"

RevOps leaders also over-index on Slack — 201% compared with the US average. But it isn't reasonable to like... go build a Slack marketing strategy.

More likely, they're using Slack because it's their company chat tool. This signals they likely work in fast-moving, cross-functional environments where internal conversations shape decisions. Unfortunately, you can't request an invite to their company Slack and start doing your marketing there (though if you can finagle that, I'd love to hear all about it).

What you can do is think about creating things worth bringing into those internal Slack conversations. The asset you create should not just be "clickable."

It should be forwardable.

Something a VP of Revenue can drop into Slack with:

"This is exactly what I've been trying to explain."

-or-

"Can we talk about this in our team meeting?"

That could be a:

- benchmark report
- calculator or diagnostic
- spicy-but-useful point of view
- teardown of a common RevOps mistake
- comparison guide that makes an internal debate easier

LinkedIn belongs in the plan. But it's not the whole strategy.

Have you considered that perhaps B2B buyers are *not* ghosting you?

NetLine's new State of B2B Content Consumption & Demand report found that in 2025, the average B2B professional took 47.7 hours to consume the content they requested, up 23.9% year over year. It also found that 45.9% of people expect to make a purchase decision within the next 12 months, while mid-term intent (6-12 months) rose 78.6%.

That feels important. A lot of marketers still interpret delayed engagement as lack of interest. But this data suggests that buyers are still researching. They're just overloaded, more cautious, and moving on longer timelines.

That also lines up with a different report from Clutch on the marketer side. Marketers are investing more in visibility, reputation, and original substance:

- 56% said they plan to produce more content this year
- 41% say reputation management is a top goal
- 27% plan to invest in proprietary research reports and white papers to improve LLM visibility.

Put those two reports together, and the takeaway is pretty clear:

B2B buyers still want content.
They're just taking longer to engage with it.
And the bar for what feels worth engaging with is going up.

## Post 6 — "Try Zero Click Marketing: start with a constraint, not a campaign"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/amandanat_if-you-want-to-try-zero-click-marketing-for-activity-7442261462473150464-QfTK
- **Date:** ~2026-04-15 (posted "2 months" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 108 likes, 48 comments

If you want to try Zero Click Marketing for yourself, don't start with a campaign. Start with a constraint.

One thing B2B growth marketer Brendan Hufford did early on was a "100 Days of SEO" project. Every day, he published a YouTube video, podcast episode, and blog post about SEO. Useful ideas, shared in public.

This is what Zero Click Marketing ultimately is. It's not one big swing — it's a body of work.

If you want to try this yourself, you don't need 100 days. But you do need structure. Here's how I'd approach it:

**1/ Pick a lane.**
Be specific. "Marketing" isn't enough. Is it... growth marketing? Ok. B2B or B2C? What sector? At what level?

**2/ Define your differentiator.**
Why you? What perspective are *you* bringing? Maybe you're a tenured operator who's done B2B growth marketing for small businesses. Or maybe you're a longtime content marketer who's pivoting into paid marketing. This one is tied closely to picking a lane.

**3/ Choose your audience.**
Write for someone you can mentor, someone you admire, and someone who's your peer. This will (hopefully) help you focus on creating content that you think is genuinely useful.

**4/ Set a cadence.**
2–3x/week is enough. Daily if you're ambitious. If it helps you to say you're publishing every Tuesday and Wednesday, go for it. But your audience doesn't need you to deliver on a specific day — they need you to deliver regularly.

**5/ Keep it standalone.**
Every post should be useful on its own without requiring a click. A post about a Meta ad campaign shouldn't require context about who you are and why you ran the campaign.

And most importantly: Don't measure this like a campaign. You're not looking for immediate conversions. You're looking for signals that your audience is paying attention.

Because by the time someone clicks, they've already made their decision.

## Post 7 — "Some LinkedIn posts feel dead on arrival"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/amandanat_some-linkedin-posts-feel-dead-on-arrival-activity-7439702164811362304-EZPX
- **Date:** ~2026-04-15 (posted "2 months" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text + image
- **Engagement:** 190 likes, 83 comments

Some LinkedIn posts feel dead on arrival. Others sit among the crickets for hours… then suddenly take off. And often, smaller accounts outperform the ones with 100k followers.

For years, most advice about growing on LinkedIn has focused on tactics like the best time to post and commenting before publishing.

But those tactics assume your post was actually selected for distribution.

After reading Christopher Penn's recent analysis of LinkedIn's engineering research, I learned the system may actually work in two stages:

1) Retrieval: deciding whether your post should even be considered for someone's feed

2) Ranking: deciding where it appears once it gets there

If your content never enters the candidate pool during retrieval, the ranking system never even gets the chance to evaluate it.

That has a big implication for marketers:

The goal isn't so much learning the algorithm. It's becoming so clear, consistent, and engaged with the right audience that the algorithm learns you.

## Post 8 — "You don't need a 40-page audience research deck"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/amandanat_good-news-you-dont-need-a-40-page-audience-activity-7401296552293756928-D3Sk
- **Date:** ~2025-12-15 (posted "6 months" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 48 likes, 19 comments

Good news: you don't need a 40-page audience research deck to pick your next content channel.

You can "guess and test" your way there. Here are two paths marketers can take:

1) Use tools (like SparkToro) to see where your audience actually hangs out

2) Or, when you don't have budget/access, run a thoughtful guess-and-test experiment instead.

What "guess and test" looks like in practice:

**Step 1: Make a reasonable guess.**
In B2B, assuming your audience is on LinkedIn is usually a safe starting point.

**Step 2: Start tiny.**
Record a short, limited-series podcast in a meeting room. Spin up a short LinkedIn series. Run a scrappy webinar. Nothing that needs a studio or a 3-month plan.

**Step 3: Watch for directional signals.**
Are the right people engaging? Do guests/partners share it? Is sales hearing "I saw your post/podcast…" on calls?

**Step 4: Decide.**
If you see traction, then invest more deeply. If you don't, kill it without guilt and try a different format or channel.

Your job isn't to be perfectly right upfront. Your job is to learn quickly enough that you know where to be excellent. (Of course... a tool like SparkToro can help you do all this a heckuva lot faster. 🙃)
