## Post 1 — "Don't start on paid until you're killing it on organic — I disagree"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/emilykramer_when-i-posted-about-paid-on-linkedin-a-few-activity-7465468921148592128-Gb1o
- **Date:** ~2026-06-01 (posted "2w" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text + diagram
- **Engagement:** 76 likes, 40 comments

When I posted about paid on LinkedIn a few weeks ago, I got several comments along the lines of "Don't start on paid until you're killing it on organic." Disagree!

This is bad advice. You should apply learnings from organic to paid and from paid to organic.

The best way to do this is to take an audience-first approach to all channels you are running, including paid.

AI makes it so much easier to have an enriched list of accounts and contacts, dynamically feed this into platforms (including LinkedIn and search), and take signals from those channels to enrich this list even more.

Build this out and all of your learnings compound. Better audience data yields better ad performance, which produces better signals, which makes audiences even better.

Here are some ways you can make sure paid learnings are driving organic learnings and vice versa:

1. SEM test → SEO focus areas. SEM is often the fastest way to test if a keyword is worth investing in on SEO and AEO.
2. De-anonymized visitors → outbound + retargeting → return visits. Play around with sequencing ads and outbound once someone visits your site (and revisits your site).
3. Closed-won → lookalikes. Build lookalikes off actual closed-won accounts, feed those into ad platforms.

More thoughts on breaking down paid silos and taking an audience-first approach in my latest newsletter: https://lnkd.in/gUDNjcgd

## Post 4 — "Marketing team data across 100 B2B startups: median = 4% of headcount"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/emilykramer_i-just-pulled-marketing-team-data-across-activity-7452421598244040704-zl-y
- **Date:** ~2026-05-15 (posted "1 month" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text + diagram
- **Engagement:** 90 likes, 28 comments

I just pulled marketing team data across 100 B2B startups. Median marketing team = 4% of company headcount.
This is lower than I expected. Or maybe just lower than I hoped.

Some quick stats from my full State of Marketing Teams Report Newsletter:

→ I've long thought 5% was a good benchmark (and fought for it when leading marketing teams). The actual median is 4%. Most teams are running leaner than the benchmark I've been preaching.

→ Your GTM motion shapes marketing team size in a big way. Self-serve companies run marketing at 5.6% of headcount. Sales-led at 3.2%. Hybrid in the middle at 4.2%.

→ Companies that sell to marketers have 2x the marketing team size of everyone else. 8.3% of headcount vs 4% for the rest of the sample. It's a small sample, but this makes a ton of sense.

→ As companies scale, marketing team % shrinks. Under 250 employees, marketing is 5%+ of headcount. Above 250, it's consistently 2-4%.

If you're benchmarking your marketing team size, 4% is the median anchor. Adjust up if you're self-serve or selling to marketers. Down if you're sales-led.

I just sent a newsletter with 30+ B2B website examples...
Because websites still matter, but you need to treat them differently.

Here's what to prioritize, with examples from each:

👬 Build for two audiences:
Humans evaluating your product and LLMs deciding whether to recommend it. Most sites aren't built for either audience well right now.

Companies doing this well: Wispr Flow, Railway, PostHog

🥇 Go beyond basic social proof to prove credibility.
Generic logo bars, quote carousels, and company stats are losing in A/B tests at a high rate. The proof that works is specific, verifiable, and hard to fake.

Companies doing this well: Clay, HeyReach, Linear

🏗️ Lower the bar to get started.
Less traffic is making it to your site. When someone does show up, they need to convert. The best sites are letting you start a project, migrate more easily, or see themselves in the product before they ever talk to sales.

Companies doing this well: Replit, Framer, Mintlify

## Post 6 — "You're likely only reaching ~50% of your audience with LinkedIn Ads"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/emilykramer_youre-likely-only-reaching-50-of-your-activity-7435373811581136896-lMiM
- **Date:** ~2026-03-15 (posted "3 months" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 69 likes, 22 comments

You're likely only reaching ~50% of your audience with LinkedIn Ads.
Why? Many people use personal emails on LinkedIn.

So, if you don't enrich contacts with personal emails + LinkedIn profile URLs, you get low match rates.

Enrichment, plus segmenting by stage and intent signals, used to be too cumbersome to do. It isn't anymore, so make it happen.

I chatted with Max Hogan, Co-Founder at Closing Media, about what the gold standard looks like right now for building matched audiences that actually match and ads that actually perform.

Here's what he told me:

+ Build audiences around signals, not just lists:
Funding events, job changes at target accounts, account stages — these should drive who's in each audience.

> Connect tools like Clay to LinkedIn via API:
Instead of uploading CSVs, audiences sync automatically. Contacts get added when signals fire and removed when they go cold or become customers.

= Segment creative for each dynamic audience:
Cold contacts see thought leadership, pipeline gets ROI messaging, customers see expansion content.

→ If your team is still uploading static lists to Campaign Manager, talk to Max.

- **Date:** ~2026-03-15 (posted "3 months" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text + image
- **Engagement:** 234 likes, 61 comments

I just wrote a newsletter for the 90% of marketers who don't have 15 agents running and aren't living inside Claude Code all day.

I wanted to create a practical onramp for marketers trying to figure this whole agent thing out. I hit send moments ago.

Here are some of my favorite tips from the newsletter:

* Start with a single, well-scoped task and a clearly defined outcome. (Not an "agent swarm")

* Write an agent job description the same way you would for a human. Define the What, When, and How of the "job" before you ever open a builder.

* Expect friction. Your first few agents will be slower and clunkier than you want. Iteration is part of the skillset. 75% of my agents haven't seen the light of day.

* Use a mix of tools on purpose: structured builders like Relay.app for repeatable workflows, autonomous agents like Claude Cowork for messy research, and productized agents like Mutiny when you want something that just works out of the box.

* The goal isn't to replace marketers. It's to give a Gen Marketer leverage so one person can execute like a small team.
