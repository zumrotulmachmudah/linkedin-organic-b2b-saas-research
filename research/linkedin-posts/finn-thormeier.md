## Post 1 — "Multi-stakeholder LinkedIn thought leadership: surgically targeting each member of the buying committee"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/finnthormeier_one-strategy-im-dying-to-try-at-an-enterprise-activity-7471171087670874113-wUvp

- **Format:** Text post

One strategy I'm DYING to try at an enterprise software / cyber company is the idea of specialized exec content, surgically distributed to individual members of the buying committee. Here's how it would work.

As an example, let's say we sell large cybersecurity deals ($100k+ ACV) into the enterprise / Fortune 1000.

Our economic buyer is the CISO, but other relevant stakeholders, influencers and blockers include the CTO, CFO, CIO, the procurement and legal team, maybe the CEO because it's strategic, and all kinds of VPs and Directors within the security team.

Let's say we build a list of 500 target accounts.

Most companies will already have cybersecurity-related content going out, that's table stakes.

But let's say we now take our own CFO and create thought leadership content from her perspective and publish it on her LinkedIn. It's not content that a CISO will find interesting, but other CFOs will.

Now we do the same thing for our own CTO, CIO, CEO, Head of Procurement, Head of Legal, VP SecOps, Director of GRC, etc.

Our CTO creates content that's relevant for other CTOs.
Our CIO creates content that's relevant for other CIOs.
Our Head of Procurement creates content that's relevant for other Procurement leaders.

They're each creating their own content, sharing their own lessons from their own world, posting it through their own LinkedIn profile, which would be relevant to THEIR peers.

Now we come back to our 500 target accounts.

We connect our CTO with the 500 CTOs at those target accounts.
We connect our Head of Legal with the 500 Heads of Legal at those target accounts.
We connect our VP SecOps with the 500 SecOps leaders at our target accounts.

As they're each sharing insights, they're building trust within that specific stakeholder of the buying committee.

We can then use LinkedIn's relatively new Thought Leader ads to GUARANTEE distribution to the right, individual decision makers at our target accounts.

The nice thing about the LinkedIn ad manager is that it TELLS you which companies are most engaged with our ads, so we know where we're resonating and can forward it to the sales team.

How do we get our CTO, CIO, Head of Legal etc to actually post regular content?

By interviewing each of them once every 2 weeks for 1 hour, host an "internal podcast" with them, ask them about their lessons and insights, and pull video clips and text posts that we prepare into almost-done LinkedIn posts for them. All they need to do is look over the posts, make a couple changes, and publish. 1h/week.

All the rest (ads, connecting, scoring, outreach) can be done by someone else.

How much faster are we building trust? How much easier will it be to overcome all the hurdles and blockers on our deals? How much better are we penetrating our target accounts than our competitors?

To me it seems like a no-brainer. No large B2B company is actually doing this. Reach out if you wanna try this.

## Post 2 — "The two types of B2B CMOs winning with AI: fully in or fully out"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/finnthormeier_ive-interviewed-many-of-best-b2b-cmos-cros-activity-7470057290721685504-ftd-
- **Date:** ~2026-06-09 (posted "6d" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 16 likes, 12 comments

I've interviewed many of best B2B CMOs, CROs and CEOs on The Executive Brand podcast. When it comes to AI, I see the winners fall into one of two archetypes:

fully in or fully out.

1. Full AI-native:

They spend most of their day inside of Claude Code or Cowork, vibecoding internal tools, building Skills/Routines, using all of the newest agentic toys, building their own agents, they have a functioning AI SDR/BDR, they're rebuilding their org chart, GTM engineering, the whole 9 yards.

Examples: Tom Wentworth, Kyle Norton

2. Going fully NOT AI:

They spend all their energy creating EXPERIENCES. Events, dinners, one-off marketing stunts, creative storytelling, all done with a unique lens and yes, taste. They just stand out. They manage to make you FEEL something, like actually.

Examples: Ariel Rubin, Don Jeter

And yes, they all have a bit of both. Tom has more taste than he's comfortable to admit. Don uses AI, but in a more "normie" way, not to automate away his design team.

The ones who I don't see winning:

Who are half in, half out.

They want to leverage AI, but they're not willing to restructure their org or rethink how they approach their GTM from the ground up. They use Claude or ChatGPT but they've never built an agent to fully own a workflow front-to-end. And they do the events and the in-person stuff, but it's kinda half-assed, they just do the same thing that everyone else does, a webinar, a conference booth with some swag. They're neither here nor there.

But that's where 90% of B2B companies live.

## Post 3 — "How Amanda Zhu grew from 1K to 52K LinkedIn followers in 12 months to help scale Recall.ai to $250M"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/finnthormeier_if-i-was-a-technical-saas-founderceo-trying-activity-7465712772048314368-I5Xp
- **Date:** ~2026-06-01 (posted "2w" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 27 likes, 10 comments

If I was a technical SaaS founder/CEO trying to build demand, I'd be studying Amanda Zhu. Without being a "natural", she grew from 1k-52k LinkedIn followers in 12 months to help her startup Recall.ai scale to a $250M+ valuation. Here's how:

For context:

- Recall is an API for meeting recordings, they sell mainly into VPs of Engineering / Product
- They did YC in 2020 and pivoted to their current product in 2022
- Before, Amanda did a CS degree and worked as a Software Engineer

How she figured out LinkedIn:

1. At the end of 2024, they did a set of customer interviews (Heads of Eng/Prod) to find out where they hang out, and LinkedIn kept coming up. Amanda feels like LinkedIn is better to reach decision makers, while X might be better to reach ICs.

2. From there, she decided to do an experiment: post every day, Mon-Fri, to see what happens.

3. The first couple of posts were ad-hoc. She just posted whatever she thought was interesting or cool. Stuff she'd want to read herself.

4. Within 2 weeks, she knew it was worth it because one of her posts went viral (100k impressions). IF she could figure it out, it was a great way to consistently get in front of her audience.

5. Amanda admits that her fast success was pure luck. But from there, you can reverse-engineer what works and shift from 90% to 50% luck.

6. I asked her, if she hadn't gone viral in the first two weeks, if she would've stuck through. Her answer: "Yes. In startups, when you build something net new, a 0 to 1 motion, it takes months if not a year."

This was the 0 to 1 motion.

Here's Amanda's process:

1. She now gets help from 1 marketer on her team.

2. They have a Notion database where they collect ideas. Can be one-liners, or a half-fleshed out post she writes down as she heads home after work, oftentimes they're voicenotes.

3. She and her marketer sit down once a week to go through all the ideas and pick 6 for the coming week, mapping them to different content pillars. During that meeting, they discuss the posts, the angle, the tone, whether they have any resources they can attach to it.

4. Her content pillars: marketing lessons, sales lessons, fundraising lessons, general founder journey, product updates.

5. In a separate meeting, usually a day later, she and her marketer co-write all 6 posts. Literally sit next to each other, both with hands on keyboard. This can take hours.

6. Once done, she schedules the 6 posts for the next week.

7. IMPORTANT: Amanda blocks 30-60min EVERY day after publishing to be active on LinkedIn. Reply to comments, answer DMs, engage with other people's content, send connection requests to prospects at target accounts. Many founders think this step is optional. It's not.

8. The LinkedIn algorithm changes all the time, so roughly once a quarter, she adapts her approach.

## Post 6 — "Semrush analyzed 89,000 LinkedIn posts cited in AI Search — here's what works for AEO"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/finnthormeier_semrush-analyzed-89000-linkedin-posts-that-activity-7447615482519822336-al63

Semrush analyzed 89,000 LinkedIn posts that were cited in AI Search (ChatGPT/Perplexity/Google AI Mode) last month. Here's what kind of LinkedIn content supports your AEO/GEO strategy:

Some stats first:

- LinkedIn is the #2 biggest citation source for LLMs (Reddit is #1, Wikipedia #3)
- 11% of all AI responses cite a LinkedIn post or article
- LinkedIn has high "semantic similarity scores", which means AI responses often mirror the meaning of your original post, so you control the narrative IF you get cited

What kind of LinkedIn content got cited:

- Educational, original content is cited most often. Long-form articles (500–2,000 words) and mid-length posts (50–299 words) account for the largest share of AI citations, and 54–64% of cited posts focus on sharing knowledge or practical advice, while reshares are rarely referenced.
- AI citations reward relevance and consistency more than virality. Most cited posts have moderate engagement (15–25 reactions), while about 75% of cited authors post frequently (5+ posts in four weeks) and nearly half have over 2,000 followers.
- You need both Company Page content and individual thought leadership. For example, Perplexity cites Company Pages most often (59%), while ChatGPT Search and Google AI Mode more often cite individual creators (59%).

How to build a LinkedIn strategy around this:

1. Pick 2-5 executives at your company who can best speak to your product, category and industry (founder, CEO, CTO, Head of CS, Head of Product etc).

2. Interview each of them every two weeks, block it off in their calendar. One hour every 2 weeks.

3. Prepare questions based on their calendar, meetings they've had, events they attended, features/products the company launched and industry announcements.

4. Turn those interviews into video clips & ghostwritten LinkedIn posts. Build a Claude skill, injecting info about your company, product, ICP, customer lingo, and that exec's tone of voice.

5. Make sure the posts are meaty, in-depth and specific. That's why you want the interview as the basis, no AI slop that generates content out of thin air.

6. Have execs review. Have them block off 30min every two weeks to batch-review all content. Once approved, you schedule it.

7. Publish 2-3 times per week per executive via their PERSONAL profile.

8. Take the best posts and publish them via your company page. Have AI rewrite it slightly into the company voice.

9. Bonus: amplify posts via Thought Leader ads to target accounts. Then use Jungler.ai and Fibbler to push post & ad engagers into Clay, enrich, qualify for ICP, push into Slack, Hubspot, use scoring to prioritize accounts for sales, and retarget via Conversation ads to drive demos.

## Post 7 — "Biggest takeaways from interviewing Tim Soulo, CMO at Ahrefs"

My biggest takeaways from my conversation with Tim Soulo, CMO at Ahrefs:

For context, Tim joined Ahrefs 10 years ago, and since then helped bootstrap the company to well over $100m ARR - with only 160 employees, no sales team & while breaking every single marketing "best practice."

Takeaways:

1. Ahrefs runs marketing with no Google Analytics installed, no attribution setup, no A/B testing, no retargeting, no discounting, no free trial, no quarterly plans, and no formal reporting. Tim's answer for how they make decisions instead? Intuition and common sense. They decide what to do based on what talent they have, what formats they're comfortable with, what makes sense, and what sounds exciting. I've done 100+ episodes with marketing leaders, and consistently, the companies that are doing the best marketing all seem to prioritize things that seem fun to them. 37signals, PostHog, Clay… Tim is another data point here.

2. Tim didn't know you were supposed to have a sales team. He just assumed people should find your website, sign up, pay (there's no free trial), and that's it. Only now, at quite a bit over $100m ARR, are they starting to build out an enterprise sales motion. There's a pattern where ignorance of the "best practices" can often lead to... better practices.

3. Instead of taking a podcast and chopping it into 15 LinkedIn posts, Tim starts with a LinkedIn post, tests the hook, reads the comments, then turns it into an article incorporating all the feedback, then combines multiple articles into a conference presentation, then discusses that presentation on a podcast. Small to big, not big to small.

4. Tim built a LinkedIn engagement tracker with Claude Code in one afternoon. It looks at his post engagements, enriches contacts through Apollo, pulls Ahrefs domain data, and shows him which companies are engaging with his content, sorted by ad spend and organic traffic. This is a CMO at a $100M+ company building his own social selling tool after lunch.

5. Every piece of content you publish should be a sales page for your product. "Thought leadership" is overrated. If you publish an article, and within that article you mention a relevant feature of your product, and it gets 10k visits, if people don't convert, that's a product problem, not a marketing problem. As a marketer, you did your job: you got the attention of relevant people and showed them something relevant. It sidesteps the attribution/ROI debate.

## Post 8 — "LinkedIn post engagement IS a valid lead signal — if your content is about the right problem"
- **Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/finnthormeier_people-with-no-idea-say-that-an-ebook-download-activity-7429848626799042560-2j1R
- **Date:** ~2026-03-15 (posted "3 months" before June 15, 2026)
- **Format:** Text post
- **Engagement:** 23 likes, 12 comments

People with no idea say that an ebook download or a like on a LinkedIn post should not count as a lead/MQL. Yes, it should. *IF* the ebook or LinkedIn post shares insights or solutions about the exact thing our product does.

Let's say we're selling a deal room software. It helps enterprise sales teams manage complex deals with many different stakeholders.

And let's say we publish a piece of content (ebook, LinkedIn post, whatever) with the headline "How to better manage complex deals if you're an enterprise AE" and someone who is an enterprise AE (or CRO) engages with that piece of content - downloads it, likes it, leaves a comment....

Should that make them a lead/MQL?

ABSOLUTELY YES.

If someone, who is our ICP, is looking for and consuming content that is sharing tips, insights, or strategies about how to solve the exact same problem that our product solves, then that should make them a lead. There's assumed intent.

The word "lead", coming from term used in criminal investigations, means there's a CLUE that should be investigated. It doesn't definitely say that this person can or will want to buy our product, but we've been tipped towards there being a decent likelihood.

The problem:

Most marketers are scored on the total AMOUNT of people who download, like, engage.

So they're incentivized to publish "The state of B2B sales" or some generic shit instead. It's a broader topic that applies to a broader set of people, so they'll get more engagement.

Yes, the person who downloads that piece of content, should ABSOLUTELY NOT be considered a lead/MQL for our deal room product.

So for god's sake, IF you want to generate business from LinkedIn, do a lot fewer selfies or opining about the future of AI, and a lot more posts talking about the problem you solve and the reasons for why your product exists.
